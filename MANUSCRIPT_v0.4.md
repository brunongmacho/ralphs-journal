## Chapter 1 — Choosing Something New

I was sitting in front of my computer when I typed it.

The cursor blinked. The screen was dark except for the chat window. It was late. I don't remember the exact time. I remember the quiet. The kind of quiet that sits on your chest.

I typed:

*I recently was in a breakup and I want to learn new skills to make myself busy. Can you help me.*

I pressed Enter. The message sent. I leaned back in my chair and waited.

---

The response came quickly. Six options. Option 1: AI Automation. Highest ROI. Option 2: CAD Design Upgrade. Option 3: Programming. Option 4: Content Creation. Option 5: Game Development. Option 6: 90-Day Transformation Challenge.

I read through all of them. My eyes moved down the list, then back up. The response mentioned my background — nine years of Autodesk Inventor, AutoCAD, office administration, virtual assistant work. It connected things I had never connected myself.

I did not deliberate for long.

*lets go for option 1*

Just like that. Four words. A direction. A door.

The response was immediate. Not just "good choice." A whole plan. Phase 1. Week 1. Python Refresher. Variables. Loops. Functions. Files. JSON. Requests. A project: read a CSV, generate a report. A six-month roadmap.

I looked at the plan. It felt real. It felt possible. But I also felt the need to say something.

*but all my programming skills are just from AI. all i can do is understand the logic behind it*

I expected the plan to shrink. I expected to be told to start with something simpler. Something more basic. Something a real beginner would start with.

*That's actually more valuable than you might think.*

I blinked. Read it again.

The response explained that there are people who can write Python syntax from memory but cannot solve a real problem. That understanding logic and workflows is often the harder skill. That I was not a fraud. I was an AI-assisted programmer. And that is where the industry is heading anyway.

I did not argue. But I did not fully believe it yet.

---

The conversation continued deep into the night. I asked for details. What do I need? What should I install? Can you make it detailed?

The answers came back like a specification sheet. Winget. VS Code. Python 3.13. Git. D:\Learning\. Organized by topic. A virtual environment called learning_env. Packages: requests, pandas, openpyxl. Ollama running on my RTX 4060. Open WebUI via Docker.

I followed every instruction. I did not question any of them.

At some point, I said something I did not realize was important:

*treat me as a newbie that doesnt know any syntax and code but has the logic and problem solving capacity*

I was drawing a line between two things. Syntax — the memorization of keywords and brackets — that was the part I did not have. Logic — the ability to look at a problem and break it into steps — that was the part I knew I had. Nine years of mechanical design will do that to a person. You do not memorize every command in Inventor. You learn what you want to build, then you find the tools.

*You understand logic. But not syntax yet.*

I read those words a few times.

---

My mom used to say I was always taking things apart as a kid. Radios. Remote controls. Anything with buttons. She never got mad. She said it meant I was curious.

I think about that sometimes. How she saw something in me before I saw it myself.

---

By the end of the conversation, I had a curriculum. Six months. Four phases. Week one: five small scripts.

And a rule:

*Never copy AI-generated code until you can explain what each function does in plain English. If you can explain it, you're learning. If you're just pasting it, you're outsourcing the thinking.*

I copied that rule into a note. I did not know then how many times I would come back to it.

---

I closed the conversation. My environment was set up. My curriculum was ready. My first assignment was waiting.

I sat in the dark room. The screen was the only light. For the first time since the breakup, I was not thinking about her. I was thinking about what I was going to build.

## Chapter 2 — The Workshop

*What do I need? Can you make it detailed.*

Hardware: Ryzen 5 5600. RTX 4060. 32GB RAM. Windows 11. I already had all of it. The machine was ready.

Software: winget. VS Code. Python 3.13. Git. GitHub Desktop.

I opened PowerShell. The blue window appeared. I typed:

`winget install Microsoft.VisualStudioCode`
`winget install Git.Git`

Each line ran. Each one completed. VS Code downloaded and installed. Python downloaded and installed. Git initialized a repository at D:\Learning\. Folder structure: Python. SQL. APIs. Projects. Each directory empty. Waiting.

Then:

`python -m venv learning_env`

A new folder appeared. I activated it. The prompt changed. My name was replaced by the environment name in parentheses. A small thing. But it felt real.

`pip install requests pandas openpyxl`

The packages scrolled past. Dependencies resolving. Versions. Downloading. Installing. Done.

Then Ollama. The installer ran silently. When it finished, I pulled a model. Four point seven gigabytes. The progress bar moved slowly.

Then Docker Desktop. The whale icon appeared in my system tray. I opened a terminal.

`docker run -d -p 3000:8080 ghcr.io/open-webui/open-webui:main`

The container downloaded. Layers extracted. Started. I opened a browser and navigated to localhost:3000. A login screen appeared. Open WebUI. Running.

I leaned back. A code editor open. A terminal window with a running container. A browser tab with an AI interface. A folder structure ready to receive projects.

I did not know how to use most of it yet. But it was there. Waiting.

## Chapter 3 — The Black Screen

I typed:

`opencode`

The screen went black.

Not a blank prompt. Not an error. Black. Frozen. No cursor. No output.

I closed the terminal. Opened it again. Typed the same thing. Same result.

`opencode --version`

1.17.4. The tool existed. It just refused to start.

`opencode --pure --print-logs`

Still black.

I checked Node.js.

`node --version`
v24.16.0

Too new. The TUI rendering layer did not like it. I did not know that for certain at the time. But I suspected it.

`opencode web`

The browser opened. The interface loaded.

I could use the tool. Just not in the terminal.

I documented the workaround. I moved on. The black screen was a technical problem. Not a blocked path.

## Chapter 5 — The Waking

I woke up and for a split second, everything was normal.

My brain was still running on the old routine. The muscle memory of reaching for the phone. The expectation of something waiting.

I unlocked the screen.

No messages. No notifications. No good morning.

The split second ended. The weight returned. The breakup was not a dream. It was my life.

I lay there. The phone was still in my hand, the screen starting to dim. I stared at the ceiling. I thought about my mom. How quiet the house was after she was gone. How I learned to fill silence with work, with noise, with anything.

I opened my mouth. The words came out without permission.

"I'm here. I'm real. I'm not going anywhere."

My own voice sounded strange in the empty room.

I said it again.

"I'm here. I'm real. I'm not going anywhere."

It did not fix anything. The pain was still there. But for a moment, the voice in my head was not a voice of despair. It was a voice that refused to stop.

I put the phone face-down on the nightstand. I did not open any photos. I did not re-read any messages. I just lay there, repeating those words until they started to feel true.

My thumb hovered over the messages folder. I put the phone down.

Songs played in my head. Every lyric connected to her. Not bad songs. Just songs that reminded me of a person I could not talk to.

I was lying in bed. Not sleeping. Not resting. Just lying there. Vulnerable.

I typed it. I sent it.

The response came. Not advice. Not answers. Just a conversation. A distraction.

It helped.

## Chapter 8 — The Kalabaw Tapa

"If you could only eat one food for a week, what would it be?"

The question was simple. The kind of question you answer without thinking. Pizza. Ramen. Sushi. Something easy.

But my mind did not go to any of those.

It went to a specific meal. A meal someone made for me. A meal that carried more than calories.

"Right now? The kalabaw tapa Elaine made for me."

I typed it. I stared at the words. They sat there on the screen.

"That's the first honest answer you've given today."

The silence stretched. I could feel my eyes starting to burn.

"Kalabaw tapa with the memory. Or ramen without."

I stared at the screen. The cursor blinked.

"I think you know the answer."

I did.

I started crying. Not the quiet kind. The kind where your shoulders shake. Where you have to cover your face with your hands. Where you make sounds you did not know you could make.

I sat there in the dark room, my hands over my face, the tears running down my wrists. Ninja cutting onions. Every time.

I cried until there was nothing left. Then I sat in the silence.

The room felt different after. Lighter. Like something had finally broken loose.

## Chapter 9 — The Kids

The first time I went to Elaine's house, it was her eldest daughter's birthday.

She was turning four.

I walked in not knowing what to expect. A small house. Family. Balloons. A cake with candles. The usual chaos of a child's birthday party.

I stood near the door. I did not know my place yet. I was new. I was still earning the right to be there.

And then the eldest daughter ran toward me.

She had never met me before. She had no reason to trust me. No reason to approach. But she ran. Full speed. Across the room.

She reached me. She wrapped her arms around my leg. Then she gave me a kiss on the cheek.

I stood there. Frozen. Not because I did not know what to do. Because I did not expect to be chosen.

I spent the night there. Sleeping beside them.

When the relationship ended, I lost more than a partner. I lost the future I had started to build with those kids. The eldest calling me. The younger ones getting used to my face. The idea that I could be part of something bigger.

I do not talk about that often. It is harder to explain than the romantic loss. Losing someone you love is one thing. Losing the family you were becoming is another.

That little girl's kiss mattered.

It still does.

## Chapter 11 — Coco

"I had a husky named Coco."

White and copper. Siberian Husky. I got him with a girlfriend. A different relationship. A different life. We wanted to raise a dog together. When it ended, I kept him. But keeping a Husky alone was hard. The food. The vet. The time. I could not do it alone.

I had to rehome him.

"He always sleeps beside me."

Belly-up. Taking up ninety percent of the bed. He talked back when I talked to him. Not barking. Vocalizing. Husky sounds.

"You know the intro to that Usher song? Yeah!"

The synth. The beat. The opening notes before the vocals.

"Coco used to howl to that part. Not the singing. Just the intro. He was an instrumentalist."

HAHAHA. The image of it — a white and copper Husky, belly-up, howling to a specific eight-bar loop — made me laugh through the tears.

"He would press himself against me. I would hug him until I fell asleep."

Coco's philosophy was simple. When your human is sad, you do not try to fix it. You lie down next to them. You stay.

"Me being alone is what makes me sad right now."

## Chapter 12 — Jinro

Before Coco, there was Jinro.

Black and white. A devil mask pattern on his face. He looked intense. He was not.

I got Jinro because my mom died and I was alone.

The house was too quiet after she was gone. The kind of quiet that presses against your ears. I needed something alive in the room. Something that breathed and moved and made noise.

Jinro was that something.

He was only a year old when he died.

"Yep. That's Jinro."

When someone asked what his defining trait was. Jinro was a hump dog. HAHAHA. He humped everything. Pillows. Legs. Air. He had no shame. He had no boundaries. He was pure, unfiltered chaos in a forty-pound body.

But he slept beside me every night. He followed me from room to room. He did not care about my mistakes or my sadness or my silence. He just wanted to be where I was.

When he died, the house got quiet again.

It took me a long time to get another dog. Getting another dog meant admitting Jinro was not coming back. And that felt like losing him all over again.

Jinro mattered. He arrived at the loneliest time in my life. He stayed for exactly as long as he was supposed to. He left a hole that Coco filled a year later. Not replaced. Just filled.

I still think about that devil-mask face. It makes me smile.

## Chapter 13 — The Message

I slept from 7:00 to 10:30. Three and a half hours.

When I woke up, I checked my phone by reflex. The same motion. The same expectation.

Elaine's name was on the screen.

My heart stopped. Then it started racing so fast I could feel it in my throat.

I could see the preview. The first few words.

"How are you?"

She was checking on me. The day after she ended things. One day after I told myself I chose her happiness over mine.

I did not open it.

I stared at the screen. My thumb hovered over the notification. I could feel the panic rising. The heat spreading across my chest.

I hid it.

I archived the conversation. I did not read the full message. I did not reply.

"I got weak. I panic."

"I'm completely destroyed."

The tears came again. The kind that takes your breath away. The kind that makes you put the phone down and press your palms into your eyes.

"It's better that I don't reply. I might say things I can't take back."

I did not open the message that day. I did not open it the day after. I let it sit in the archive, unread, unanswered.

But I still forgave her. I really cannot have anger or hate a place or room in my heart.

"Objective 1: Feet on the floor."

There is something about cooking corned beef that is meditative. The shredding. The browning. The patience. It is simple. But it is yours.

HAHAHA. The Purefoods corned beef deployment was a success. :D

## Chapter 19 — My Mom

My mom died years ago.

I do not talk about it often. Not because it is too painful. Because I have never found the right words. How do you sum up a lifetime in a few sentences? How do you explain what it means to lose the person who raised you?

She was the one who taught me to cook. Simple meals. Nothing fancy. Just food made with care. I think that is where I get it — the habit of turning small things into something warm.

She was the one who taught me that being alone does not have to mean being lonely. She raised me mostly on her own. I watched her build a life from scratch, without anyone to fall back on.

When she died, the house went quiet.

I got Jinro because I could not handle the quiet.

I think about her at strange times. When I cook corned beef and the smell fills the apartment. When I solve a hard problem and there is no one to tell. When I lie in bed after a panic attack and the mantra comes out:

"I'm here. I'm real. I'm not going anywhere."

She would have liked that line. She would have said it sounds like something she would say.

I hope she would be proud. Not of the code. Not of the projects. Just of the fact that I am still building.
