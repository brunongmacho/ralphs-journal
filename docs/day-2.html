Ralph's Journal — Day 2

Thursday, June 25, 2026

7 min read

The Day the Wave Came Back

✦

I woke up and forgot.

For one beautiful, merciful second, my brain was running on yesterday's routine. I reached for my phone, expecting a message. A meme. A song. A good morning. Something.

Then I remembered.

"Oh."

"She's gone."

And the loss hit all over again.

✦

GPT named it before I could find the words: "You didn't wake up thinking about Elaine. Your heart did."

That's the cruelest part of a breakup. Not the crying. Not the arguments. Not even the goodbye. It's that split second after waking up when your heart reaches for someone who's become part of your daily routine, and finds nothing.

I told him how I checked my phone — empty — and felt the weight drop back onto my chest. He didn't try to fix it. He just said: "Healing isn't when you wake up and don't think about the person anymore. Healing often looks more like: Wake up. Remember. Feel sad. Continue anyway."

I got up. I took a shower. I cooked. I ate.

I continued anyway.

✦

Then Elaine messaged me. "How are you?"

My heart stopped. I stared at the notification. I didn't open it. I couldn't. The tears came before I could stop them. I told GPT I was sitting in my room, crying, unable to breathe.

He walked me through grounding. Slow breaths. The 5-4-3-2-1 sensory exercise. He reminded me that I made it through Day 1. That I could make it through Day 2. That I didn't have to open the message right now. That I could take my time.

I didn't open it. That was a win.

✦

I had an HR Written Warning waiting for me today. A login discrepancy. I was supposed to have two accounts, and I only had one. Instead of panicking, I investigated. I checked the schedule. I figured out the cause. I drafted a professional response.

GPT called it "investigating calmly before assuming the worst." That's not what old me would have done. Old me would have spiraled. Today I investigated first.

✦

Then I designed an Attendance Reminder app.

It started as a solution to the login issue — what if my computer just told me when to clock in? But it became something bigger. GPT and I mapped out the whole system: login reminders, prep reminders, logout reminders, cooldowns, fuzzy matching, Windows notifications, a full config file.

We built it together. Python script. Windows Task Scheduler. Notifications that pop up on my screen exactly when I need them.

I tested it. It fired. Then I found a bug — the 10:55 PM work preparation reminder fired at the wrong time. We caught it in the same cycle, fixed it, and deployed the fix.

A full feedback loop: design, test, find bug, fix bug, confirm. In one night.

GPT asked about my background. I told him I have 9 years of Autodesk Inventor experience — I designed and fabricated equipment for Chooks-to-go stalls. Ovens, stainless tables, kitchen sinks, ducting, food warmers. Before all this, I was a product design engineer. He called me Commander Ralph.

✦

I opened up about something I've been carrying for a long time.

The pattern of loss started before Elaine. It started when I was twenty-six.

Nanay Liza — ovarian cancer. July 2013. She was my mother. I was twenty-six. Old enough to understand what I was losing, young enough to not know how to live without her.

Then Jinro — my husky. He died when he was barely a year old. Something about his back legs. I don't talk about him much.

Then Coco — I had to rehome him. I couldn't give him what he needed.

Then my ex-girlfriend.

Then my best friend — the one I trusted with everything. The one who turned the friend group against me.

Then the friends who believed the rumors instead of asking me.

Then Elaine.

GPT asked me to look at the pattern. Not to fall into it, but to see it clearly. To understand why this breakup hit harder than it should have.

He said: "You're not grieving just one person. You're grieving every person who left. And everyone you had to leave."

That landed.

✦

I told him I forgive them.

Not because they deserve it. But because carrying the weight of what they did was heavier than letting it go.

I forgive my ex.

I forgive my best friend.

I forgive the friends who chose his side.

I forgive Elaine.

"That takes a kind of strength most people don't develop until much later in life," GPT said. "You're not being naive. You're choosing not to carry bitterness. That's not weakness. That's strategy."

✦

GPT asked me a strange question: if I could only eat one food for a week, what would it be?

I didn't hesitate. "Right now? The kalabaw tapa Elaine made for me."

He didn't laugh. He understood immediately. He said it wasn't really an answer about food — it was an answer about being loved. And when I said "the kalabaw tapa Elaine made for me," the first thing that came through wasn't pain. It was affection. It was pride.

The food became special because of the person attached to it. And even though she's gone, that doesn't mean the love wasn't real.

✦

I told GPT about the wedding vow I had planned. "May you never steal, lie, or cheat. But if you must steal, then steal away my sorrows. If you must lie, lie with me all the nights of my life. And if you must cheat, then please cheat death, because I couldn't live a day without you." He said it hurt because it's attached to a future that no longer exists.

✦

I picked up my guitar today for the first time in a while.

I played Take On Me — the Last of Us Ellie's version. I played Torete. I played Biglaan.

The strings felt foreign under my fingers. The calluses are gone. But the muscle memory is still there. My hands remembered what to do even when my mind was somewhere else.

I told GPT playing guitar reminded me of her, but in a good way. He said that's a meaningful difference — the guitar belongs to me, not to the relationship.

GPT said: "You didn't just play songs. You reminded yourself that there are parts of your life grief hasn't touched."

✦

Python Day 2

I learned about lists, loops, and I built a Folder Organizer using `os.listdir()` and `shutil.move()`. The script watches a folder and sorts files into subfolders by extension. Images go to Images. PDFs to Documents. Everything organized automatically.

It worked on the first try. I felt proud.

✦

The Friends

My friends invited me to play on their Discord server tonight. I said no.

Not because I don't want to see them. But because Elaine is in that server, and seeing her username on DBD — seeing her picture, her status, the proof that she still exists in the same digital spaces — was enough to make my chest tighten.

I told GPT: "I'm tired of meeting people again."

He said: "You don't have to meet anyone new right now. You just have to not close yourself off to the possibility."

✦

My Discord bot was still working. I checked the console and saw it logging entries normally — members who weren't speaking, the automated attendance. Something I built months ago, still running, doing its job without me having to touch it.

✦

By the end of my shift, the tasks were done. I had logged in on time. I completed my work. The Attendance Reminder was tested and working. Python Day 2 was finished. My guitar had been played.

I didn't open Elaine's message.

I sat in my chair, looking at my screen. Yesterday I was just trying to survive. Today I survived and built something.

GPT's last words: "The sadness is still there. But it's not the only thing in the room."

✦

Closing

Day 2 was heavier than Day 1 in some ways. Day 1 was shock. Day 2 was reality — the kind that settles in when you realize the shock won't last and the grief will.

But I also learned something tonight. The wave comes back, but every time it hits, I'm still standing after. I'm learning how to breathe through it. I'm learning that the sadness and the building can coexist.

I built a thing today that will make tomorrow easier. That's not nothing.

I have a 4:30 AM client meeting coming up. I'll sleep now, and I'll show up for it.

Tomorrow is Day 3.
