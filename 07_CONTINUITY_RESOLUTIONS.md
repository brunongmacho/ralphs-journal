Add a call-forward in Ch. 1 ("Later that night, GPT laid out the full map — six months, five milestones, one philosopher's rule.") so the reader knows the plan exists without breaking the emotional pacing of Part I.

### Confidence Level

## ISSUE 2: The Dream (Chapter 21)

### Original Evidence
- **Day3.txt L3757:** `me: i think i cannot sleep because of what happened. i am tired and drained yes. but i dont feel any sleep coming as of now`
- **Day3.txt L4272:** `me: my eyes kinda dropping down. i think i want to rest for a while`
- **Day3.txt L4375:** `**System entering rest mode.**`
- **Day3.txt continues until line 4379:** `Sleep well, my friend. We'll keep building tomorrow. 💙🚀`
- **END OF FILE.** Ralph goes to sleep. No follow-up conversation about what he dreamed.

### Key Finding
**The dream does NOT appear in any of the three chat files.** The old manuscript (Ralph.txt lines 666-701) contains a dream sequence that is not sourced from Ralph's actual words in any captured session.

This means one of two things:
1. The dream was created as literary license during the previous manuscript's writing process.
2. The dream was mentioned in a session or medium not captured in these files.

### Reasoning
Without direct source evidence, I cannot confirm Ralph dreamed of Elaine. The scene in the old manuscript is beautifully written but may not be historically accurate. However, the emotional logic is sound: Ralph goes to sleep after a panic attack triggered by Elaine's nickname removal, and it is entirely plausible — even likely — that he dreamed of her. The key constraint: we do not have Ralph's own words saying "I dreamt of her."

### Decision
**Two options — Editor-in-Chief must choose:**

- **Option A (Pure Accuracy):** Exclude the dream chapter entirely. Chapter 21 becomes an epilogue chapter titled "Tomorrow" that covers Ralph waking up, the aftermath of the panic, and the morning after. The mantra repeats as a promise. The book ends with "This isn't the end. It's only Volume I."

- **Option B (Emotional Truth with Honest Framing):** Include the dream but preface it with an author's note that makes the source transparent:
  > *"What follows is a reconstruction. I fell asleep after the panic. I don't remember exactly what I dreamed. But I know what my heart was carrying. And I know what I felt when I woke up."*
  Then proceed with the emotional truth of the scene — the waking, the silence, the realization that healing isn't finished.

**My recommendation: Option B.** The dream is the emotional culmination of three days of grief. Excluding it creates a structural void in Part V. But framing it honestly preserves the memoir's commitment to accuracy.

### Confidence Level
**Low-Medium (60%)** on the dream being real. **High (95%)** on the emotional truth being correct regardless. The reader needs SOME resolution after the panic attack in Chapter 20.

### Remaining Ambiguity
Whether Ralph actually dreamed of Elaine is unconfirmed. The emotional arc demands it. The source cannot confirm it.
